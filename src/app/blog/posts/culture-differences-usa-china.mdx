## The Cafeteria Clap

One of the first things that caught me completely off guard in the US was the **cafeteria clap**. Back in China, if you accidentally dropped a cup or a plate in a public dining area, people would either pretend nothing happened or maybe give you a sympathetic glance.

In America? The entire cafeteria erupts in applause. The first time it happened to me, I dropped a cup and suddenly heard clapping from all directions. I thought I had done something impressive! It took me a moment to realize it was a playful, lighthearted reaction — almost like a friendly way of saying *"hey, we've all been there!"*

Now I can't help but smile every time I hear that collective clap. It's one of those small, silly traditions that makes you feel like you're part of something, even when you've just embarrassed yourself.

## "Bless You!"

In China, sneezing in public is just... sneezing. Nobody really reacts to it. But in the US, the moment you sneeze, at least one person nearby will say **"Bless you!"** — sometimes even a complete stranger.

The first time someone said "Bless you" to me, I was confused. *Why are they blessing me? Did something bad happen?* I later learned it's just a polite tradition, and honestly, it's one of the warmest little customs. It makes you feel noticed and cared for, even in passing.

## Other Small Surprises

- **Tipping culture**: In China, tipping can actually be seen as rude. In the US, it's expected — and I had to learn the 15-20% rule fast!
- **Personal space**: Americans generally keep more distance when talking. In China, standing close is completely normal and even shows friendliness.
- **Small talk**: Americans love chatting about the weather with strangers. In China, people tend to keep to themselves unless they know you.
- **Ice water everywhere**: Every restaurant in the US gives you a giant glass of ice water. In China, we drink hot water — even in summer!

## What I've Learned

Moving from China to the United States has been one of the biggest adventures of my life. These little cultural differences used to confuse me, but now they make me appreciate both cultures even more. Every country has its own way of showing care, humor, and community — you just have to be open to seeing it.

The world is a lot more fun when you can laugh at a cafeteria clap and feel warmed by a stranger's "Bless you."
